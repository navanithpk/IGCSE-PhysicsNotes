A student has a measuring cylinder and a small, irregularly shaped piece of metal. The piece of metal can easily fit into the measuring cylinder.
Describe how the student can use the measuring cylinder and some water to find the volume of the metal.

1. Pour some water into the measuring cylinder and record the volume of the water.
2. Carefully lower the piece of metal into the water, ensuring it is fully submerged and no air bubbles are trapped.
3. Record the new volume of the water.
4. The volume of the metal is the difference between the two volume readings.

A device for measuring gas pressure is connected to a gas supply as shown in the diagram.
Suggest why this measuring device uses mercury rather than coloured water.

Mercury is much denser than water, so a shorter column of mercury is needed to measure the same pressure as a taller column of water. This allows for a more compact and practical measuring device.

The volume of a block is 0.0089 m3. The mass of the block is 70 kg.
Calculate the density of the block.

Using the equation for density, where *ρ* is density, *m* is mass and *V* is volume: $$\rho = \frac{m}{V} = \frac{70 \text{ kg}}{0.0089 \text{ m}^3} = \boxed{7900 \text{ kg/m}^3}$$

Question 4

A bowl contains 500 cm3 of water at a temperature of 5.0 °C. The bowl of water is placed in a freezer for several hours. When the bowl is removed from the freezer, it contains ice at a temperature of –18.0 °C. The density of water is 1000 kg/m3.

**(a)** Calculate the mass of water in the bowl when it is placed in the freezer.

First, convert the volume of water from cm3 to m3: $$500 \text{ cm}^3 = 500 \times 10^{-6} \text{ m}^3 = 5.0 \times 10^{-4} \text{ m}^3$$ Using the equation for density, where *ρ* is density, *m* is mass and *V* is volume: $$m = \rho V = 1000 \text{ kg/m}^3 \times 5.0 \times 10^{-4} \text{ m}^3 = \boxed{0.50 \text{ kg}}$$
