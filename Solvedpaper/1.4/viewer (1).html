A student has a measuring cylinder and a small, irregularly shaped piece of metal. The piece of metal can easily fit into the measuring cylinder.
Describe how the student can use the measuring cylinder and some water to find the volume of the metal.

1. Pour some water into the measuring cylinder and record the volume of the water.
2. Carefully lower the piece of metal into the water, ensuring it is fully submerged and no air bubbles are trapped.
3. Record the new volume of the water.
4. The volume of the metal is the difference between the two volume readings.

Suggest why this measuring device uses mercury rather than coloured water.

Mercury is much denser than water, so a shorter column of mercury is needed to measure the same pressure as a taller column of water. This allows for a more compact and practical measuring device.

The volume of a block is 0.0089 m3. The mass of the block is 70 kg.
Calculate the density of the block.

Using the equation for density, where *ρ* is density, *m* is mass and *V* is volume: $$\rho = \frac{m}{V} = \frac{70 \text{ kg}}{0.0089 \text{ m}^3} = \boxed{7900 \text{ kg/m}^3}$$

A bowl contains 500 cm3 of water at a temperature of 5.0 °C. The bowl of water is placed in a freezer for several hours. When the bowl is removed from the freezer, it contains ice at a temperature of –18.0 °C. The density of water is 1000 kg/m3.

**(a)** Calculate the mass of water in the bowl when it is placed in the freezer.

First, convert the volume of water from cm3 to m3: $$500 \text{ cm}^3 = 500 \times 10^{-6} \text{ m}^3 = 5.0 \times 10^{-4} \text{ m}^3$$ Using the equation for density, where *ρ* is density, *m* is mass and *V* is volume: $$m = \rho V = 1000 \text{ kg/m}^3 \times 5.0 \times 10^{-4} \text{ m}^3 = \boxed{0.50 \text{ kg}}$$

**(b)** The specific heat capacity of water is 4200 J/(kg °C). The specific heat capacity of ice is 2100 J/(kg °C). The specific latent heat of fusion of water is 3.3 × 105 J/kg.
Calculate the energy given out as the water cools from 5.0 °C to ice at –18.0 °C.

The energy given out can be calculated in three steps: 1. **Cooling the water from 5.0 °C to 0 °C:** * Using the equation for energy transfer during temperature change, where *Q* is energy, *m* is mass, *c* is specific heat capacity, and *ΔT* is the change in temperature: $$Q_1 = mcΔT = 0.50 \text{ kg} \times 4200 \text{ J/(kg °C)} \times (5.0 - 0) \text{ °C} = 10500 \text{ J}$$ 2. **Freezing the water at 0 °C:** * Using the equation for energy transfer during a change of state, where *Q* is energy, *m* is mass, and *L* is the specific latent heat: $$Q_2 = mL = 0.50 \text{ kg} \times 3.3 \times 10^5 \text{ J/kg} = 165000 \text{ J}$$ 3. **Cooling the ice from 0 °C to –18.0 °C:** * Using the equation for energy transfer during temperature change, where *Q* is energy, *m* is mass, *c* is specific heat capacity, and *ΔT* is the change in temperature: $$Q_3 = mcΔT = 0.50 \text{ kg} \times 2100 \text{ J/(kg °C)} \times (0 - (-18.0)) \text{ °C} = 18900 \text{ J}$$ Therefore, the total energy given out is: $$Q = Q_1 + Q_2 + Q_3 = 10500 \text{ J} + 165000 \text{ J} + 18900 \text{ J} = \boxed{194400 \text{ J}}$$

A student has a measuring cylinder, a beaker of liquid and a balance.
Describe how the student can use this equipment to determine the density of the liquid.

1. **Measure the mass of the empty beaker:** Place the empty beaker on the balance and record its mass.
2. **Pour some liquid into the beaker:** Pour some of the liquid from the beaker into the measuring cylinder and record its volume.
3. **Measure the mass of the beaker and liquid:** Place the beaker with the liquid on the balance and record its mass.
4. **Calculate the mass of the liquid:** Subtract the mass of the empty beaker from the mass of the beaker and liquid.
5. **Calculate the density of the liquid:** Using the equation for density, where *ρ* is density, *m* is mass and *V* is volume, divide the mass of the liquid by its volume.

A metal block has a mass of 86 g and a volume of 8.0 cm3.

**(a)** Calculate the density *ρ* of the metal using the equation.

Using the equation for density, where *ρ* is density, *m* is mass and *V* is volume: $$\rho = \frac{m}{V} = \frac{86 \text{ g}}{8.0 \text{ cm}^3} = \boxed{10.75 \text{ g/cm}^3}$$

**(b)** The metal block is placed in some liquid. The metal block floats on the liquid.
Suggest a value for the density of the liquid.

For the metal block to float on the liquid, the density of the liquid must be greater than the density of the metal block. Therefore, a possible value for the density of the liquid is **11 g/cm3**.

A sample of sand has a volume of 0.050 m3. The density of the sand is 1900 kg/m3. The specific heat capacity of the sand is 1500 J/(kg °C).

**(a)** Calculate the mass of the sample of sand.

Using the equation for density, where *ρ* is density, *m* is mass and *V* is volume: $$m = \rho V = 1900 \text{ kg/m}^3 \times 0.050 \text{ m}^3 = \boxed{95 \text{ kg}}$$

**(b)** Calculate the thermal capacity of the sample of sand.

Using the equation for thermal capacity, where *C* is thermal capacity, *m* is mass, and *c* is specific heat capacity: $$C = mc = 95 \text{ kg} \times 1500 \text{ J/(kg °C)} = \boxed{142500 \text{ J/°C}}$$
