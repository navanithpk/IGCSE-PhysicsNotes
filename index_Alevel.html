Physical Quantities

A physical quantity is a property of a material or system that can be quantified by measurement. It consists of a numerical magnitude and a unit. For example, the length of a table is a physical quantity. It might have a magnitude of 1.5 and a unit of meters.

Random errors and systematic errors

Systematic errors are errors that are consistent in magnitude and sign. They are caused by problems with the measuring instrument or the experimental technique. Systematic errors affect the accuracy of a measurement but not the precision.

  • Zero error is a type of systematic error that occurs when a measuring instrument does not read zero when it should. For example, if a balance reads 0.1 g when it is empty, all measurements taken with the balance will be 0.1 g too high.
  • Parallax error is another type of systematic error that occurs when a measurement is taken from an angle, rather than from directly in front of the scale. This can happen when reading a ruler, a thermometer, or any other measuring instrument with a scale.

Random errors are errors that are not consistent in magnitude and sign. They are caused by unpredictable variations in the measurement process. Random errors affect the precision of a measurement but not the accuracy. Examples of random errors include:

  • Variations in the environment, such as temperature or air pressure.
  • Human error, such as misreading a scale or timing an event incorrectly.

Nuclear Physics

Alpha Decay (\(\alpha\)-decay)

In alpha decay, a nucleus emits an alpha particle (two protons and two neutrons), represented as:

\[ {}^{A}_{Z}X \rightarrow {}^{A-4}_{Z-2}Y + {}^{4}_{2}\alpha \]

Example: Uranium-238 decays into Thorium-234 by emitting an alpha particle:

\[ {}^{238}_{92}U \rightarrow {}^{234}_{90}Th + {}^{4}_{2}\alpha \]

Beta Minus Decay (\(\beta^{-}\)-decay)

In beta-minus decay, a neutron is converted into a proton, emitting an electron and an antineutrino:

\[ {}^{A}_{Z}X \rightarrow {}^{A}_{Z+1}Y + \beta^{-} + \bar{\nu}_e \]

Example: Carbon-14 decays into Nitrogen-14:

\[ {}^{14}_{6}C \rightarrow {}^{14}_{7}N + \beta^{-} + \bar{\nu}_e \]

Beta Plus Decay (\(\beta^{+}\)-decay)

In beta-plus decay, a proton is converted into a neutron, emitting a positron and a neutrino:

\[ {}^{A}_{Z}X \rightarrow {}^{A}_{Z-1}Y + \beta^{+} + \nu_e \]

Example: Fluorine-18 decays into Oxygen-18:

\[ {}^{18}_{9}F \rightarrow {}^{18}_{8}O + \beta^{+} + \nu_e \]

Gamma Decay (\(\gamma\)-decay)

In gamma decay, an excited nucleus releases energy by emitting a gamma ray (\(\gamma\)), without changing the number of protons or neutrons:

\[ {}^{A}_{Z}X^* \rightarrow {}^{A}_{Z}X + \gamma \]

Example: Cobalt-60 emits gamma radiation:

\[ {}^{60}_{27}Co^* \rightarrow {}^{60}_{27}Co + \gamma \]

Mass-Energy Equivalence

The energy released in nuclear reactions can be calculated using Einstein's equation:

\[ E = mc^2 \]

Where:

  • \( E \) = energy (J)
  • \( m \) = mass defect (kg)
  • \( c \) = speed of light (\( 3 \times 10^8 \, \text{ms}^{-1} \))

Cosmology

Hubble's Law

Hubble's Law states the relationship between the recession velocity of a galaxy and its distance from Earth:

\[ v = H_0 \cdot d \]

Where:

  • \( v \) = recession velocity of the galaxy (ms\(^{-1}\))
  • \( H_0 \) = Hubble constant (kms\(^{-1}\)Mpc\(^{-1}\))
  • \( d \) = distance to the galaxy (Mpc)
