Cambridge A-Level Physics Notes

Section 1: Physical Quantities and Units

1.1 Physical Quantities

  • A physical quantity consists of a **numerical magnitude** and a **unit**.
  • It's important to make **reasonable estimates** of physical quantities.

1.2 SI Units

  • **Base quantities** are fundamental quantities that cannot be defined in terms of other quantities. The following are the SI base quantities and their units:
    • **Mass:** kilogram (kg)
    • **Length:** meter (m)
    • **Time:** second (s)
    • **Current:** ampere (A)
    • **Temperature:** kelvin (K)
  • **Derived units** are expressed as products or quotients of base units. For example:
    • **Velocity:** m/s (meters per second)
    • **Force:** kg·m/s2 (kilogram meters per second squared), also known as a Newton (N)
  • **Prefixes** are used to indicate decimal submultiples or multiples of both base and derived units. The following prefixes and their symbols should be recalled and used:
    • pico (p), 10-12
    • nano (n), 10-9
    • micro (μ), 10-6
    • milli (m), 10-3
    • centi (c), 10-2
    • deci (d), 10-1
    • kilo (k), 103
    • mega (M), 106
    • giga (G), 109
    • tera (T), 1012
  • **Homogeneity of physical equations** can be checked using SI base units. For example, in the equation for momentum (momentum = mass × velocity), the units on both sides of the equation should be the same.

1.3 Errors and Uncertainties

  • **Systematic errors** are consistent errors that affect all measurements in the same way. Examples include:
    • **Zero errors:** occur when a measuring instrument doesn't start at zero.
    • **Parallax errors:** occur when measurements are read at an angle.
  • **Random errors** are unpredictable errors that cause individual measurements to vary.
  • **Precision** refers to how close repeated measurements are to each other.
  • **Accuracy** refers to how close a measurement is to the true value.
  • **Absolute uncertainty** is the amount by which a measurement may be incorrect.
  • **Percentage uncertainty** is the absolute uncertainty expressed as a percentage of the measurement.
  • The uncertainty in a **derived quantity** can be assessed by adding the absolute or percentage uncertainties of the individual measurements.

1.4 Scalars and Vectors

  • **Scalar quantities** have only magnitude. Examples include: distance, speed, volume, mass, work done, energy, temperature, charge, voltage, and frequency.
  • **Vector quantities** have both magnitude and direction. Examples include: displacement, velocity, acceleration, momentum, force, and torque.
  • **Adding vectors:**
    • Vectors in the same direction can be added directly.
    • Vectors in opposite directions are subtracted.
    • **Graphically:** using the head-to-tail method or parallelogram method.
    • **Mathematically:** using Pythagoras' theorem for perpendicular vectors and trigonometry for vectors at any angle.
  • **Resolving vectors:** involves breaking a vector down into its horizontal and vertical components using trigonometry.

Section 2: Kinematics

2.1 Equations of Motion

  • **Distance** is the total length traveled.
  • **Displacement** is the change in position from the starting point to the ending point.
  • **Speed** is the rate at which distance is covered.
  • **Velocity** is the rate of change of displacement (speed in a given direction).
  • **Acceleration** is the rate of change of velocity.
  • **Graphical representation:**
    • **Displacement-time graphs:**
      • Gradient represents velocity.
      • Can have negative gradients.
    • **Velocity-time graphs:**
      • Gradient represents acceleration.
      • Area under the curve represents displacement.
    • **Distance-time graphs:**
      • Can only have positive gradients.
  • **Equations of motion (suvat equations):** are derived from the definitions of velocity and acceleration and apply to uniformly accelerated motion in a straight line.
    • \( v = u + at \)
    • \( s = ut + \frac{1}{2}at^2 \)
    • \( v^2 = u^2 + 2as \)
    • \( s = \frac{1}{2}(u + v)t \)
    Where:
    • \(s\): displacement
    • \(u\): initial velocity
    • \(v\): final velocity
    • \(a\): acceleration
    • \(t\): time
  • **Free fall:** is motion under the influence of gravity alone (ignoring air resistance). In this case, the acceleration is the acceleration due to gravity, \(g\), which is approximately 9.81 m/s2.
  • **Projectile motion:** occurs when an object is launched into the air and follows a parabolic path.
    • The horizontal and vertical components of velocity are independent of each other.
    • Horizontal velocity remains constant.
    • Vertical velocity changes due to gravity.

Section 3: Dynamics

3.1 Momentum and Newton's Laws of Motion

  • **Mass** is the property of an object that resists changes in motion (inertia).
  • **Newton's First Law of Motion:** An object at rest stays at rest and an object in motion stays in motion with the same speed and in the same direction unless acted upon by an unbalanced force.
  • **Newton's Second Law of Motion:** The rate of change of momentum of an object is directly proportional to the resultant force acting on it and is in the same direction as the force.
    • This can be expressed mathematically as \(F = ma\), where:
      • \(F\): resultant force
      • \(m\): mass
      • \(a\): acceleration
    • The unit of force is the Newton (N), which is defined as 1 kg·m/s2.
  • **Weight** is the force of gravity on an object and is calculated using \(W = mg\), where:
    • \(W\): weight
    • \(m\): mass
    • \(g\): acceleration due to gravity
  • **Newton's Third Law of Motion:** When two bodies interact, they apply forces to one another that are equal in magnitude and opposite in direction. These forces are of the same type.

3.2 Non-Uniform Motion

  • **Frictional forces** and **viscous/drag forces** (including air resistance) oppose motion.
  • When an object moves against a resistive force, it may reach a **terminal velocity**, where the resistive force equals the driving force, resulting in zero net force and constant velocity.

3.3 Linear Momentum and its Conservation

  • **Linear momentum (p)** is the product of mass and velocity: \(p = mv\).
  • **Impulse** is the change in momentum of an object.
    • It can be calculated as the product of force and time: Impulse = \(Ft\).
  • **Principle of conservation of momentum:** The total momentum of a closed system remains constant, provided no external forces act on the system. This applies to both elastic and inelastic collisions, as well as explosions.
  • **Elastic collision:** kinetic energy is conserved.
  • **Inelastic collision:** kinetic energy is not conserved.

Section 4: Forces, Density, and Pressure

4.1 Turning Effects of Forces

  • **Centre of gravity** is the point where the entire weight of an object appears to act.
  • **Moment of a force (torque)** is the turning effect of a force about a pivot and is calculated using \(moment = force × perpendicular distance from the pivot\).
  • **Couple** is a pair of forces that are equal in magnitude, opposite in direction, and parallel to each other, but not along the same line of action.
    • Couples produce only rotation, not linear motion.
    • The **torque of a couple** is calculated using \(torque = force × perpendicular distance between the forces\).

4.2 Equilibrium of Forces

  • **Principle of moments:** For a system in equilibrium, the sum of the clockwise moments about any point equals the sum of the counterclockwise moments about the same point.
  • **Equilibrium:** exists when there is no resultant force and no resultant torque acting on an object.
  • **Vector triangle:** can be used to represent coplanar forces in equilibrium.

4.3 Density and Pressure

  • **Density (ρ)** is defined as mass per unit volume: \(ρ = m/V\).
  • **Pressure (P)** is defined as force per unit area: \(P = F/A\).
  • **Hydrostatic pressure** is the pressure exerted by a fluid at rest due to its weight and is calculated using \(ΔP = ρgΔh\), where:
    • \(ΔP\): change in pressure
    • \(ρ\): density of the fluid
    • \(g\): acceleration due to gravity
    • \(Δh\): change in height
  • **Upthrust** is the upward force exerted on an object submerged in a fluid due to the difference in hydrostatic pressure between the top and bottom of the object.
    • **Archimedes' principle:** states that the upthrust on an object submerged in a fluid is equal to the weight of the fluid displaced by the object.
    • Upthrust can be calculated using \(F = ρgV\), where:
      • \(F\): upthrust
      • \(ρ\): density of the fluid
      • \(g\): acceleration due to gravity
      • \(V\): volume of fluid displaced

Section 5: Work, Energy, and Power

5.1 Work and Energy Conservation

  • **Work done (W)** is the product of force and displacement in the direction of the force: \(W = Fs\).
  • **Gravitational potential energy (ΔEp)** is the energy an object possesses due to its position in a gravitational field.
    • It is calculated using \(ΔE_p = mgΔh\), where:
      • \(ΔE_p\): change in gravitational potential energy
      • \(m\): mass
      • \(g\): acceleration due to gravity
      • \(Δh\): change in height
  • **Kinetic energy (Ek)** is the energy an object possesses due to its motion.
    • It is calculated using \(E_k = \frac{1}{2}mv^2\), where:
      • \(E_k\): kinetic energy
      • \(m\): mass
      • \(v\): velocity
  • **Principle of conservation of energy:** Energy cannot be created or destroyed, only transferred from one form to another.
  • **Efficiency** of a system is the ratio of useful energy output to total energy input.

5.2 Power

  • **Power (P)** is the rate at which work is done or energy is transferred.
    • It can be calculated using:
      • \(P = W/t\): work done per unit time
      • \(P = Fv\): force times velocity

Section 6: Density, Pressure, and Deformation of Solids

6.1 Stress and Strain

  • **Deformation** is a change in shape or size caused by tensile or compressive forces.
  • **Tensile forces** pull an object, causing extension.
  • **Compressive forces** push an object, causing compression.
  • **Hooke's Law:** states that the extension of a spring is directly proportional to the force applied, provided the limit of proportionality is not exceeded.
    • This can be expressed mathematically as \(F = kx\), where:
      • \(F\): force
      • \(k\): spring constant
      • \(x\): extension
  • **Elastic deformation:** the object returns to its original shape when the force is removed.
  • **Plastic deformation:** the object does not return to its original shape when the force is removed.
  • **Elastic limit:** the point beyond which permanent deformation occurs.
  • **Stress (σ)** is defined as force per unit area: \(σ = F/A\).
    • It has the same units as pressure (Pascals, Pa).
  • **Strain (ε)** is defined as the extension per unit original length: \(ε = ΔL/L\).
    • It is a dimensionless quantity and can be expressed as a decimal or a percentage.
  • **Young modulus (E)** is a measure of a material's stiffness and is defined as the ratio of stress to strain: \(E = σ/ε\).
    • It has the same units as stress (Pascals, Pa).

6.2 Elastic and Plastic Behaviour

  • The area under a force-extension graph represents the work done in deforming a material.
  • **Elastic potential energy (Ep)** is the energy stored in a deformed elastic material.
    • It can be calculated using:
      • \(E_p = \frac{1}{2}Fx\): for a material deformed within its limit of proportionality
      • \(E_p = \frac{1}{2}kx^2\): for a spring
